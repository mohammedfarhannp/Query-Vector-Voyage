Japan and Mount Fuji – The Sacred Peak
-----
Mount Fuji, or Fujisan as it is known in Japanese, is the tallest mountain in Japan, standing at 3,776 meters (12,389 feet). Located on Honshu Island, about 100 kilometers southwest of Tokyo, this active stratovolcano has erupted multiple times, with the last eruption occurring in 1707 during the Edo period. Mount Fuji is not just a geographical landmark; it holds deep spiritual and cultural significance in Japanese society. For centuries, it has been considered a sacred mountain and a symbol of perfection, beauty, and resilience. The perfectly symmetrical cone, often capped with snow for several months of the year, has inspired countless artists, poets, and writers. Perhaps the most famous artistic depiction is Hokusai's series "Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji," which includes the internationally recognized print "The Great Wave off Kanagawa." Climbing Mount Fuji is a popular pilgrimage activity, with the official climbing season running from early July to early September. Thousands of climbers ascend each year, many beginning their climb at night to reach the summit in time for the famous sunrise, known as Goraikō. The mountain is surrounded by five beautiful lakes – Lake Kawaguchi, Lake Yamanaka, Lake Sai, Lake Motosu, and Lake Shoji – which offer stunning reflections of the peak. Mount Fuji was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2013, recognized not only for its natural beauty but also for its profound influence on Japanese art, religion, and culture. Whether viewed from a speeding bullet train, a traditional hot spring, or the summit itself, Mount Fuji remains an enduring symbol of Japan's natural majesty and spiritual heart.
